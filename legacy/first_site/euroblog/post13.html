Back at it again in Amsterdam

Surprise, it's not Alex

Posted by Diane Lin Apr 4, 2016

Hello Alex's readers! I'm probably one of the only few non-family members to read this blog and since I'm also in Europe and sometimes traveling with him, we thought it would be cool to put in a post I wrote about the weekend in Amsterdam. To be honest, I was a little reluctant to go to Amsterdam again at first seeing as how I had been there less than a year ago with my family. But by Thursday afternoon I still had no plans for the weekend and was discussing things with Ferrara and it ended up being that Amsterdam was the most reasonable place for both of us to go to, considering ticket prices (for me, obviously not for him since he has a Eurail pass), distances, and how last minute it was. Not to say that the plan we eventually formulated was reasonable. It was just the most reasonable of all the plans we could’ve made because we were #procrastinators

Right off the bat things weren’t going great because I got a text around 8pm on Friday saying that my bus, originally due to leave at 22:45 (side note: I love military time because there is never any confusion as to when exactly I booked a ticket for) had been delayed for an hour an a half. I still got to the bus stop on time though because I’m paranoid, but that turned out to not be so bad because I made a friend waiting there- it was this Master’s student from China who was also in Amsterdam just for a weekend. We chatted about what it was like to live in Germany as foreigners and what we liked and didn’t like about Europe. The delay passed by pretty quickly because I was loving the fact that I could speak in Chinese to him. Living in a small German town hasn’t really presented me with many chances to practice my second language, so being able to converse with him was pretty nice.

Anyway, the bus finally arrived and we got on it. I fell asleep almost immediately, and was happy to see when I woke up that we were practically there. I have gotten really good at sleeping on public transportation lately, which is nice when you have a 9 hour bus ride because it goes by just like that. I got off the bus and found wifi in a Starbucks (say all you want about mass-consumerism and overpriced burnt coffees but when I’m in a foreign country with no internet trying to find friends, this place is honestly a refuge for me), messaged Ferrara, and found out that we were getting into two completely different train stations. Oops. But it turned out to be okay, because he and our friend Jimmy both had those wonderfully convenient railway passes, and were able to get to my station in 5 minutes where we met up, to go right back to the train station they originally came from- Centraal.

One of the many wonderful things about Amsterdam is that it’s super walkable. Not only is it a relatively small city, it’s also one of the prettiest I’ve been to in Europe because of the water. It’s a lot like Venice (which is where its nickname, Venice of the North, comes from) but somehow more real. Something about the smell of the canals in Venice and the fact that the water is dyed a jarring turquoise makes it less idyllic to me. Venice is more or less a tourist trap; Amsterdam at least has culture. But anyway, we walked around and Ferrara and Jimmy, true to their American roots, stopped in a McDonald’s and got breakfast. We found our way to the main square and hopped onto a free walking tour, which I was wary about at first (the tour guide said that it was 3 hours long and there was no dropping out. Of course we could probably sneak away but now I’d feel a lot shittier doing it) but it turned out to be one of the best decisions we made all day (there were not that many of them).

The tour guide was originally from Germany, I think, and spent her time giving tours in Amsterdam and Portugal. She was well-spoken and very straightforward and I liked her a lot. She told us that the name Amsterdam came from the fact that the main river running through it was called Amster and the people built a dam on it (now that I think about it, that name could not be more self-explanatory) and that the whole city was basically just built on water, which is crazy if you think about it. This was why all the buildings were a little crooked and leaning because the ground we were standing on was always moving a little from the ocean underneath. She said that the city was a huge trading area back in the day and that it became such a liberal city because there were constantly other trading companies from different countries coming in and out, bringing their cultures and people and products with them. This is also where the universal acceptance for the red-light district and coffee shops came from- as long as people enjoy them and they aren’t harmful and (most importantly) they bring in revenue, then no one could care less about their taboo nature.

Somehow this attitude really, really vibed with me. Being pretty liberal myself, I’d always thought that people should be free to do anything they wanted as long as it wasn’t harming others or themselves. I never understood the idea that one person should be so hell-bent on controlling another person, especially if it doesn’t affect them (which is why I’m pro-choice and pro-marriage equality and everything). Just because you don't agree with something someone does, that doesn't give you the right to control their actions. People should be free to smoke weed if they want to as long as they aren’t negatively affecting others. People should be free to do whatever they want with their bodies because it’s their life. You do you, I’ll do me. Amsterdam was all about this attitude and as a result, I was all about Amsterdam.

We walked around some more, saw the smallest house in the city (taxes were based on how wide houses were back in the day, so this house was literally as wide as a window), ate some cheese (aged gouda, mmm), talked about Rembrandt (but not about Vermeer, who was better in my opinion), and ended our tour at the Anne Frank house. I tipped her €10 which was pretty high for me, because I thought she did a damn good job. Somehow she was able to explain the history and make it interesting for me, someone who finds history really boring, because she was able to bring it back to modern day times and tie it in with the unique culture and people. She turned the city into a story and gave it depth and made my visit seem more meaningful. So yeah, I tipped her a lot considering it was a free walking tour.

After the walking tour, the three of us checked out the inside of a smoke shop (crazy packed, understandably smokey) and a magic mushroom shop. Then we walked to the museum plaza next and bought some lunch- I got a veggie burger and they got sloppy joes and hot dogs. After lunch we walked to a nearby park which was a lot like Piedmont in Atlanta, but prettier and busier. There were tons of people just hanging out on the grass, laying on blankets, playing music, despite the fact that it was 50 degrees and cloudy; I could only imagine what it was like in the summer. We decided to leave soon after and found ourselves in a museum gift shop, where we had a marvelous time looking at the postcards and pictures and at the holographic books and playing with the tinker toys sets and stuff.

Unfortunately, the museum closed at 6pm so we had to leave soon after getting there. We made our way to a busy street with an H&M (I needed warm clothes; it was a lot colder than I thought it was going to be). I bought a cardigan and a long-sleeved shirt while the guys ate €5 Chinese food next door. The rest of the night was pretty uneventful, it was a series of wandering around, having a beer, eating food, and wandering around some more. We didn’t have a hostel and the next trains and buses back were in the morning so we had to stay out all night. Remember when I said that our plan wasn’t very reasonable? Yeah, that's why. One cool thing was that we got to see the red light district at night, though. Obviously when I was with my family I didn’t get to see it. Honestly, it wasn’t as ratchet as I had pictured. There weren’t prostitutes walking around or anything, like I've seen in Prague. There were just gorgeous women in bikinis or lingerie standing in windows, backlit by the infamous red LED lights, beckoning to strangers on the street. From what our tour guide had told us, it was a really regulated industry. And apparently there were security buttons in every room that, if pressed, would bring police there in less than a minute. We witnessed a few guys going in and the curtains closing and they would walk out shortly thereafter. Allegedly the rate is €50/15 minutes but apparently the average time in one of those rooms is only 6 minutes. And if you’re not careful, you’ll end up getting scammed and having to pay extra for stuff. It’s a pretty legit business.

Since we were all pretty tired by the time the early AM hours rolled around, we found ourselves sitting on a ledge outside the train station, falling asleep. We decided to nap in a nearby McDonald’s and on the way there poor Jimmy threw up the Belgium fries, two pizza slices, and wok he’d consumed that night. We were all kind of mess but it was nice to see, when we got to McDonald’s, that we weren’t the only ones wanting to nap there. Eventually it was time to go home, so we all took the train back to my station and we parted ways. I got on the bus and was dead asleep by the time it left. We had a nice little layover in Wurzburg and got back to my apartment in Stuttgart around 6pm, where I went for a run, made dinner, and slept again. All in all, it was an exhausting weekend but I think Amsterdam has become one of my favorite cities, if not my absolute favorite, in Europe.
