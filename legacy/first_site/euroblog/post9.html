Interlaken, Switzerland

Posted by Alexander Ferrara Mar 9, 2016

I have some pretty mixed feelings about Switzerland as I haven’t exactly been a huge fan of certain aspects of the culture there, but there is good reason that it got 3 pics on my Instagram this week and it was difficult to narrow it down even to that – Switzerland is beautiful. I should rephrase that really, the Alps are beautiful. On the way to Interlaken we passed through a number of small cities and towns in the Alps and all of them are mind-blowingly scenic. It isn’t fair to all of the other gorgeous places in the world, because you just can’t compete with how nice the Alps are. Every time I have been around them they reclaim their throne atop my list of most beautiful places in the world.

The City

Anyways, as usual I was planning on leaving super early on a train but it didn’t happen. I went a bit (way) too hard on Thursday night and couldn’t pull myself out of bed until almost noon to get on a train, so I ended up getting to Interlaken pretty late in the afternoon on Friday. If you don’t know the basic geography around Interlaken check the map below – it is a smaller city centered between large lakes to the east and west and more or less completely surrounded by mountains. When you are downtown in the city you have almost a 360 degree of mountain peaks all around you which is awesome. In the Google drive pics you will see a number of pictures of the same exact view at different times/days which is the view from my hostel room window and it was awesome. There is also a pic that you can see a table and window in the foreground, which was the pretty insane view from the dining area of the hostel.

Interlaken was very small, everything within walking distance with relevant ease. Lots of cool little stores – primarily American brands. There was a sweet looking Patagonia store that I tried to go to a couple of times but their hours were super weird and inconvenient so I never made it in. Directly in the center of Interlaken right across from our hostel, for the first time that I have seen in Europe, there was a Hooters. It was cool stumbling upon it since it only exists here in a couple of countries, very sparsely so at that and it just seems so American. Unfortunately I never made it there to eat (mostly since it was still hella expensive because Switzerland) so I can’t speak to their quality, but I was just happy to see wings on a menu in Europe.

If you didn’t know as well, Interlaken is considered to be the Adventure Capital of Europe. The advertising everywhere was all for extreme sports encompassing everything from skiing to sky diving. They are probably most known for paragliding, and I definitely saw why. From the first walk from the train station to the hostel we were constantly seeing paragliders. It wasn’t even all that expensive to try it given what a unique experience it was, but noone else in my travel group shared that sentiment and I definitely would have preferred some company. In the end it probably didn’t matter that much since the weather was so unfavorable on Saturday that I believe most of the extreme sport places were closed anyways – have to get it next time. Everything here was pretty extreme though, even some weirder ones like minigolf. Our hostel came with this card that got us free bus rides, pool access, and minigolf so we were joking all weekend about going to play minigolf but never made it there. On Sunday on our way to the train station we stopped just to see the minigolf course that never was and you will see a good number of pictures of it because it was crazy. Cement paths, jumps that you have to make, half pipes with holes halfway up, it looked like it was designed based on the sketches of a 14 year old boy with XTreme written all over it. Definitely cool though, would have been fun to try it esp since it was free.

Overall the city (as well as the trip as a whole) was very quiet. It was a nice change of pace from all of the big city weekends to be able to just not hear a bustling city around me. We definitely got the vibe that this was a summer town, and given that it is the middle of winter it was pretty deserted. There was barely anyone there who seemed like locals, or even Europeans for that matter. I have seen a lot of Asian tourists everywhere that I go, but Interlaken probably takes the cake for having the most which given the lack of locals presence meant a huge ratio and seemingly even larger presence. The nightlife was also pretty quiet and nearly nonexistent here – tbh some of the most fun we had at night since everything closes around 6:30 was as 6 adults playing on a playground. Granted the playgrounds in Europe in general are way less concerned with safety than the US and thus have way cooler stuff... and a little pregaming never hurt.

The Hostel

So I mentioned the hostel a couple of times - it was easily the best one that I have stayed in. Granted, Switzerland, so it wasn’t cheap, but it was also pretty worth it for the #6 ranked hostel in the world and the #1 ranked hostel in Switzerland 6 years running. It was extremely conveniently located just across a park from the town center – the same park that most of the paragliders were landing in too so that was cool.

Hiking

We spent most of the day on Saturday hiking in the mountains around Lauterbrunnen. If you zoom in on that area on the map below you can get a good idea of where we were hiking. It was a nice slow 12 or so miles in the snow with plenty of breaks for dumb fun snow activities. It also went through a couple of mountain towns where people were casually wearing skiis around which was pretty funny. The weather was very not stellar, with pretty moderate to heavy snowfall most of the day and the fog way too thick to even see the surrounding mountains. Oh speaking of not being able to see mountains, Interlaken kind of backs up to one really big vertical face that everyone paraglides off of and at night it is so isolated with such little ambient light that it gets dark enough that you can’t even see it aside from the large star-shaped light at the top. Had we had Sunday’s weather for the hike on Saturday that would have been absolutely perfect with clear skies and incredible views, but the hiking was still very chill and scenic in its own way. One thing that was kind of cool about Switzerland is the lack of security and general trusting feeling from all of the people. For instance in one of the towns we went through on our hike had an “Honesty Store” which was literally an unmanned store that you could take what you wanted and just stick the payment for it in a box on the counter. I feel like there aren’t many communities where something like that could actually be functional.

Lakes

On Friday when I got there I had walked to one of the lakes to see it and while it was already dark when I got there it was definitely still a sight to behold. On Sunday with the great weather I decided to go back and see that same lake in the daylight, followed by a bus to see the other bordering lake. This is where Switzerland really gets some cred for beautiful sights. Yes, the Alps being there definitely are a significant factor, but there a lot of awesome lakes in Switzerland right next to those mountains with the bluest, clearest waters that makes for just an incredible view. I took tons of pictures of these, so you shouldn’t have any shortage to see what I am talking about.

Negatives?

I didn’t know what an appropriate title for this was, but I pretty much have included everything that I wanted to say, and ranting in my last post was fun so I figured I would do that again about Switzerland. A lot of things about Switzerland seem to be made really inconvenient and just feed this superiority vibe that everything Swiss gives off. Pretty much all of the following stem from them not being in the EU too, like get your shit together and join literally every country around you. Between the perceived superiority complex and everything being propriety to Switzerland, it is basically the Apple of Europe. For instance:

  • They use their own currency. Sure, you all are so wealthy that $5 needs to be a coin for you, but just use euros like all of the other countries around you so that we don’t have to exchange currency because that is just an unnecessary pain in the ass. Only redeeming factor is that almost everywhere here took cards and having a card without foreign transaction fees made that pretty convenient.
  • Most of the trains here don’t accept Eurail passes. That is dumb, as all of the bordering countries accept it for 99% of their regional trains, so why are yours too good for it?
  • There are no deals with other mobile phone carriers apparently. Free mobile has had included roaming coverage everywhere else that I have been in Europe. Sprint Global roaming has had at least 2G coverage effectively everywhere in Europe. Switzerland? Nope. Additional fees for Free and non-existent for Sprint.

Other Stuff

I had some down time last night to work on some coding personal projects so that was fun. Umm that’s about it for my life right now. Pics are shared where they normally are: https://goo.gl/6Nc2Gw .
