Austria


Posted by Alexander Ferrara Feb 3, 2016

While I have posted pics, a lot of them won’t make a lot of sense without explanation, so I’m going to write this with the pictures pulled up beside it and just follow along with those. Thus I would recommend reading it as such if you want the pictures explained. (Trip to Austria folder)

It takes a really long time to get to Austria by train - in the ballpark of like 15 hours, so we made the necessary stop on the way to the station for the staples: beer, baguettes and pound cake. You can get a really long pound cake here for like 1.25 euros so they have become a pretty significant part of my diet here. Our ride there only had a couple of connections in Saarbruecken and Mannheim. The pictures through the Nalgene bottle sized beer are all from Saarbruecken. It was a pretty nice looking shopping area. Gotta love anytime that you get off of a train and can immediately see McDonald’s, Burger King, KFC. More and more this Americanized trend is continuing when it comes to shopping. All of the store names must not mean much to the locals as I doubt that GameSto, Footlocker and the like translate the same way to any other language, but I’m not one to complain about seeing English. I’m also not sure what the J in TJMaxx stands for, but here it is TKMaxx so I’d love to know what that subtle change is for. I’m not sure what H&M is, but the people here must really love that and Swarovski stuff because those are by far the most common stores that you see in every shopping area that I have been to in Europe. Last note on stores, the convenience store in the Saarbruecken train station was awesome because it was open when we were there after 10pm (everything here closes around 7) and had the cheapest alcohol I have ever seen in the world. You are in a magical place when the beer is literally cheaper than the water in both stores and at restaurants and beer cans come by the liter. I love Germany. Oh and the mannequins wearing morph suits were super creepy. Germans are weird.

We had another hour or twoish layover in Mannheim so walked around there as well. We had a pretty funny encounter with some locals here. It was around midnight and we were walking down a pretty narrow sidewalk when some guys who seemed to be lower class German folk who had been and were currently drinking blocked the sidewalk, probably trying to mess with the tourists. They let Jimmy pass because they liked that he was walking around with a liter beer in his hand and we all quickly became friends when the rest of us were able to produce beers from our bags. I will retweet my earlier statement that Germans are weird, and I love it. We hung out with them for a little while talking. They told us about how they love the US military and want us to intervene more over here with ISIS and stuff which was surprising. They also said that they really like both Obama and George Bush though so take from that what you will. Granted they did say that I looked like Sheldon Cooper and seemed surprised that I didn’t have aspergers, but they were nice guys. There is a pic of a map of Mannheim there as well which is so oddly satisfying - beautifully laid out.

When the pics become daytime welcome to Vienna, Austria. The American Pizza place I liked because the subheading was about NY burgers and grill which doesn’t even sound like pizza at all. The Hofer pic is there just because it was Aldi but rebranded for a different country. I don’t particularly care about Aldi but I know @Kelsy likes it and she reads this and sees this pics too so shoutout I guess. The next bunch of pics are of Schonbrunn Palace and the gardens and stuff behind it. Unfortunately I didn’t get time to explore the inside of the building and see the room where Motzart played his first ever concert at age 6, and everything in the garden was super dormant because winter, but the grounds were still really cool and the view from the top of the hill in the gardens was pretty sweet. Oh and there was a zoo in the gardens. Some guy was rich and powerful enough to say “Hey, I know what to do with all of my people’s money and probably free labor - get some Pandas and Giraffes and shit in my backyard. It’ll go great right next to the hedge labyrinths.”

You’ll see multiple pics in here of restaurants named Gasthaus because throwback. Didn’t eat at any of those specifically, but the food that I did have here could have literally come from our Gasthaus Tirol back home. I guess that it makes sense since the staff was mostly Austrian but it was some nice nostalgia going on. The cathedral downtown here was another really nice one and had some really cool history inside. I’ll let you read the Wikipedia page for yourself though, no need for me to retype it. The view of Vienna from here was also really neat. I love how in one view you can see so much classic architecture in the city as it clashes with the colored lights and beauty of modern buildings in the shopping areas. The picture of a menu description for Slipper Pale Ale is worth reading, most convincing menu item description I have ever read and it was actually the best beer I have probably ever had.

Next up are some pics that look like an angry protest. It was a little uneasy walking around the city during the day when we started seeing the police force in mass all over the central district dressed in full riot gear. So many cops everywhere (3000 according to the news) you turned very well armed it was all very eerie. We ended up stumbling upon where the protest was going on at one point that night so obviously had to get a closer look. Everyone had signs about Nazis and were carrying red flags and stuff so we asked some protesters what they were all about. Tl;dr right-wing group/neo-nazis were having a ball so people were protesting and then there were neo-nazis also protesting the protest so it was all a big mess. It was a pretty interesting experience though with a lot of masked people shouting and chanting and stuff. Most of the group followed along the march for a while to see what happened but I ended up separated from the group and then the police wouldn’t let me past their barricades again which they had at every intersection anywhere near the march route. Austria is weird too I guess. Pretty chill night after that, Vienna has some cool nightlife and then hit that 5am train to Innsbruck.

Somehow I have ended up 1200 words in and not even skiing yet. I woke up and all of the mountains had appeared everywhere that you looked out of either side of the train. Do a google image search for Innsbruck if you want an idea of how gorgeous the backdrop of the mountains on the town is. We dropped off our stuff at our 4-star hotel for the weekend (not exactly living the hostel life) and picked up our rental gear from the rental place. Shoutout to Die Boerse, the employees there were super friendly and helpful. Day one we hit Patscherkofel I think, it definitely started with a P and that one looks familiar. It was the sight of the Men’s Downhill Skiing even when the Olympics were there. The 6 of our group who knew what they were doing went straight to the slopes while the 6 others got our feet wet with the bunny slopes. That was really easy, so obviously we were ready to hit the real Olympic slopes. Let’s just say that their idea of beginner is very very very different from my idea of beginner. We were so unprepared for what we had gotten ourselves into and it was a very difficult and dangerous experience for those of us who sucked. I actually did hit a guy which wasn’t good. Granted he was absolutely right in saying that no one around me was safe, but my favorite quote from his long tirade at me was “This isn’t the Rockies, kid” after learning that I was from the US. In my defense though, this was [supposedly] a beginner slope and he was a total dick.

Day 2 we skied at Axamer Lizu, the site of all of the other Olympic alpine skiing courses (1964, 1976). This day it was snowing pretty intensely all day and the fog/clouds/haze/whatever it was so dense that you could barely see the mountainline around you from the bottom of the slopes. The snow here was soo much better though, way more fresh powder than day 1. They had a super beginner slope here too, but it was much longer and wider and as someone who sucks it was still pretty fun even though it was really easy. Having learned my lesson from day 1 I…. went right back to the real slopes. The fog/haze/clouds/whatever was so dense up here that all you could see in every direction was white. The edges of the pistes were marked with reflector poles, and if you got close you would see why as they were pretty much just cliffs on both sides. So I can’t really ski, I can’t see the edges, if I go off the path I go off a cliff, and just as a bonus most of the pistes around the one that I was on were closed due to high avalanche warning. You can imagine how that went. Fear of dying in 10 different ways there made it go about as poorly as it could have, but hey Alps.

I was the only one with our group of 12 trying to make it back to for my 8am class the next morning, so I had to leave the mountain a couple of hours early to start my 15 hours adventure back home alone. It was aight, a bit stressful with some tight connections and late trains but I got back eventually. Being alone was kind of nice in that it forced me to meet some more locals - like the German military guy with actual vampire teeth who was a surprisingly nice seeming guy. I met this one guy on a train going to my 12-4am layover who was from Alaska/Florida/Texas but has lived in Germany now for 5 years. He was pretty chill and showed me some places around the train station for food and stuff. I had planned on just sleeping in the train station, but the waiting room was this freezing cold room with the only other person there being a homeless guy that I had encountered on my way out to Austria. I elected to grab some McDonalds and then found the only other bench in the train station. Not 2 minutes later a pretty crazy seeming homeless woman came in shouting at(?) those of us on the bench angrily in German. She didn’t really seem like she was talking to us though, but rather reciting something loudly in our direction. It was weird so I went to a bar for a bit to wait it out.

When I returned to the train station around 1:30 she was still going ranting at the people on the bench so I decided that I was ready to hit up that bench in the waiting room next to the homeless guy who was at least sleeping. I woke up around 4:15 and went to look for my bus to the next train station. There was only one bus there, but no real signage if it was the right one. After a failed attempt to communicate with the driver I just decided to go with it and got on. The entire ride I was the only person on the entire XL charter bus which was neat I guess. Got back to Metz around 5:50, my room around 6:20 and got like a bonus hour of sleep before class, awesome I made it. I get to class and we are all there at 8am on a Monday like good students when another prof comes in to inform us that our class was canceled. I’m not even going to rant about that, I think that it speaks for itself. Normally I would love class to be cancelled, but not after leaving the Alps early to take the worst set of trains ever alone to ensure I was back in time for a class - that was cancelled.

Other than that things that I forgot. The Austrian countryside is as beautiful as the German countryside. It is like an entirely different world, would be really interesting to live there. The McDonalds in Austria and Germany both have the same awesome chili sauce on their McChicken’s that Amsterdam had - step your game up America. One of the last pictures refers to a bread party in my room. I’m not going to explain it but yeah that’s bread all over my floor. The train station picture at the end is to show how you can’t see anything with the weather that day as compared to the same pic earlier the day before where you see tons of mountains there.

The moment you’ve all been waiting for –

US, France, Netherlands, Luxembourg, Germany, Austria => 6 #cultured
